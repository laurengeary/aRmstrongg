Introduction

To determine the effect of length on human forearm flexor force generation and how fatigue affects the force length (FL) relationship, maximum force measurements at 12 angles will be taken before the upper limb muscles are fatigued (control condition) and then after they are fatigued (fatigued condition). Previous research on the changes in the quadriceps muscle following eccentric fatigue shows that there was a significant shift in the peak torque angle to longer muscle lengths in the eccentrically-exercised leg immediately and on the following two days after exercise when compared with baseline (Yeung and Yeung 2007).This relationship was also reported in Butterfield and Herzog (2005) which found eccentric exercise has been shown to have a measurable effect on the forcelength relationship (FLR), as peak force is shifted to longer muscle lengths following exercise. We will apply this concept to determine if eccentric fatigue shifts the force length relationship of human forearm flexors. We will then analyze class-wide force-angle data for isometric MVC and see if it follows a typical force length relationship both under control and fatigue conditions. Finally, we will analyze the data to see if there is a significant shift in max between the control and fatigue FL relationships.

Methods

The group began by constructing a low-cost load-cell force DAQ system. With this we measured the force of isometric MVCs for ~15 seconds at 12 different angles that were determined using a handmade ganiometer. Then we eccentrically fatigue biceps by repeatedly performing iso-kinetic controlled drops with a heavy book bag for 3 minutes. After resting for 2 minutes we remeasured the force of isometric MVCs at the same 12 angles using the same procedure. We performed this on each of the three group members. After inserting our data from our Arduino sketch into a class directory, we read in the class data and calculated normalized values. We fit different polynomial models to our normalized data and then used AICs to find the best fitting model. Using the model we were able to find the Fmax and max. By comparing the two max between our two data sets, we were able to determine if there was a significant shift between max between the control and fatigue FL relationships.

Discussion

The P value of the anova test is 0.006778 which is < 0.05. This suggests that there is a statistically significant difference in the theta_max value between the control and fatigue conditions. Using the best models, the mean.shift and se.shift was calculated. The mean.shift calculated was 20.20721 and the standard error was 6.844911. This indicates that the theta_max is shifted 20.20721 degrees greater in the fatigue condition than in the control condition. The graph of the normalized maximum forces vs angle for both the control and fatigue condition follows the expected isometric force-angle curvature illustrated in the Module 7 tutorial using simulated data. In addition, the graph indicates that the fatigue curve peak is right shifted compared to the control curve peak as expected. These findings are consistent with the findings of Yeung and Yeung (2007) and Butterfield and Herzog (2005) that the peak torque angle increases following fatigue of the muscle. A seperate study found that eccentric exercise was accompanied by a shift in optimum angle for torque generation which offers further support to the results of the class data analysis as well as the other studies referenced (Whitehead et al. 2001). Yeung and Yeung (2007) suggests that the shift in optimum angle for force generation may be a protective strategy against injury.
